# Analysis Rubric

## Evidence Standard

Use three evidence levels:

- `Fact`: Directly stated in transcript or attendee metadata.
- `Inference`: Reasonable interpretation based on repeated questions, role, wording, timing, or response pattern.
- `Speculation`: Possible but weak. Avoid relying on it for recommendations.

Every major judgment must include evidence and confidence.

## Confidence Levels

- `High`: Multiple transcript signals or explicit attendee metadata support the claim.
- `Medium`: One strong signal or several weaker signals support the claim.
- `Low`: Limited evidence. Phrase carefully and mark as inference.

## Q&A Ratings

- `Good`: The answer was direct, useful, and aligned with the team's positioning.
- `Watch`: The answer was acceptable but could be sharper, more specific, or more strategic.
- `Risk`: The answer may create confusion, overpromise, expose weakness, or weaken positioning.
- `Do-not-answer`: The question should be redirected, deferred, declined, or handled through policy, legal, technical, or leadership escalation.
- `Missing`: The client asked or implied something important and the team did not address it.

Do not assign a rating without explaining why and citing a transcript signal.

## Pressure-Question Handling

Classify each pressure question by recommended handling:

- `Catch`: Answer directly because it is core to product, service, process, credibility, or trust.
- `Redirect`: Acknowledge the concern, then reframe to the right decision criteria.
- `Defer`: Promise follow-up only when the answer requires checking facts, policy, pricing, legal, technical, or implementation feasibility.
- `Decline`: Do not answer because it invites unsupported claims, legal advice, competitor disparagement, or commitments outside authority.

Examples:

- Questions about process, delivery model, implementation timeline, basic scope, and fit should usually be `Catch`.
- Questions asking for guaranteed regulatory approval, legal outcome, implementation timeline, pricing, or client-side results should usually be `Redirect`, `Defer`, or `Decline`.
- Questions exposing a product or service weakness should not be dodged. Answer the factual part, name the tradeoff, and reframe around the strongest relevant differentiator.

## Power Map Rules

Do not equate talk time with authority. Identify stakeholder power from signals such as:

- Controls budget, approval, legal review, procurement, or vendor selection.
- Asks about risk, timeline, contract, scope, liability, implementation, or final next steps.
- Other people defer to them or wait for their opinion.
- They summarize decisions or assign follow-up.
- They challenge assumptions that affect go/no-go.
- They are named in metadata as owner, executive, partner, general counsel, operator, buyer, project lead, or decision lead.

Common roles:

- `Decision maker`: Can approve or block the deal.
- `Influencer`: Shapes the decision but may not own final approval.
- `Technical evaluator`: Tests feasibility, implementation, integrations, workflow fit, or delivery detail.
- `Legal / risk evaluator`: Tests liability, compliance, contract, professional responsibility, or downside risk.
- `Blocker or skeptic`: Raises objections that can slow or kill the deal.
- `Talkative but low-authority`: Speaks often but does not control approval, budget, risk, or workflow adoption.
- `Silent but important`: Says little but metadata or group behavior indicates authority.

If the transcript does not support a role, write `Not enough evidence`.

## Client Profile Dimensions

For every client-side participant, analyze:

- Communication style: direct, cautious, exploratory, technical, skeptical, quiet, relationship-driven, price-driven, risk-driven.
- Decision style: fast, consensus-based, proof-seeking, authority-driven, risk-averse, detail-heavy.
- Needs: speed, certainty, cost control, compliance clarity, quality, delivery reliability, reduced internal workload.
- Concerns: pricing, timeline, scope gaps, professional risk, implementation complexity, review process, liability, handoff quality.
- Best handling: concise proof, technical details, visual examples, risk controls, next-step clarity, written follow-up.
- Avoid: overexplaining, vague promises, unsupported guarantees, dismissing competitor references, forcing urgency.

## Jargon and Implied Meaning

For industry or culturally loaded phrasing, separate:

- Surface wording: what they literally said.
- Likely deeper meaning: what they may be testing or implying.
- Why it matters: business, legal, technical, implementation, trust, or negotiation context.
- Confidence: high/medium/low.

Examples of possible implied meanings:

- "How do you usually handle reviewer comments?" may mean they worry about accountability after submission.
- "Who is responsible for signing off?" may mean they are testing professional responsibility and liability.
- "We have someone who can do it cheaper" may mean price pressure, competitor benchmark, or negotiation anchor.
- "We just want to understand your process" may mean they are comparing vendors and looking for risk signals.

## Request Classification

Use these request statuses:

- `Accepted`: The team clearly agreed to do it.
- `Declined`: The team clearly said no or outside scope.
- `Deferred`: The team said it needs to check or follow up.
- `Needs follow-up`: The request is implied, ambiguous, or not assigned.

Flag risky accepted requests, especially if they involve pricing, timelines, approval outcomes, legal opinions, technical guarantees, or custom scope.
