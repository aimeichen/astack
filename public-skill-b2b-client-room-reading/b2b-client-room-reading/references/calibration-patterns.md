# Calibration Patterns

Use these anonymized patterns to interpret high-consideration B2B calls. They are designed for AEC, legal, professional services, technical sales, and other expert-heavy industries.

Do not paste raw transcript excerpts, customer names, addresses, project names, or sensitive client data into this file.

## Pattern: Hands-On Internal Champion, Not Final Buyer

Signals:

- The participant personally used the product, edited a client-facing deliverable, found workflow gaps, and plans to brief an owner, partner, or executive.
- They ask detailed questions about output quality, editability, branding, source validity, pricing assumptions, or implementation handoff.

Likely meaning:

- This person may not approve the purchase, but they can strongly influence trust and adoption.

Recommended handling:

- Treat them as a practical evaluator and internal champion.
- Give them an executive-ready summary with ROI, limitations, and next-step options.
- Solve workflow blockers before pushing a larger contract.

Confidence rule:

- High confidence when they describe firsthand use and internal briefing responsibility.

Do-not-say:

- Do not dismiss them because they are not the final buyer.

## Pattern: Editable Deliverable as Conversion Blocker

Signals:

- The client says the output cannot be sent as-is.
- The client asks for editable files, branding, section selection, hidden pricing, redlines, or custom language.
- The client manually edits or recreates output before sharing externally.

Likely meaning:

- The product or service value may be proven, but repeat usage depends on last-mile output control.

Recommended handling:

- Clarify current export and editing options.
- Separate internal analysis from client-facing deliverables.
- Ask which fields, sections, claims, or assumptions must be editable.

Confidence rule:

- High confidence when the client withholds or modifies output before sharing.

Do-not-say:

- Do not imply a static deliverable is enough for all professional users.

## Pattern: Data Freshness as Trust Breaker

Signals:

- The client finds a source mismatch, policy change, reviewer preference, or operational detail that differs from the output.
- The client asks how current the database, research, or source material is.
- The client distinguishes public-source information from field-level or reviewer-level reality.

Likely meaning:

- The client is testing liability and trust, not just asking a technical question.

Recommended handling:

- Acknowledge that public or official sources can lag real-world practice.
- Use confidence labels and identify high-impact items for human verification.
- Offer to revise the affected deliverable and explain the correction loop.

Confidence rule:

- High confidence when the client provides a specific source mismatch.

Do-not-say:

- Do not say information is always guaranteed current.

## Pattern: One-Off Paid User Resisting Annual Conversion

Signals:

- The client likes the output and keeps buying individual deliverables.
- The client frames annual or subscription pricing as only worthwhile at sufficient volume.
- The client compares retail vs wholesale economics.

Likely meaning:

- Product value is accepted, but annual conversion requires usage math and workflow fit.

Recommended handling:

- Provide a break-even table.
- Ask for monthly qualified leads, deliverable usage, time saved, and close or conversion impact.
- Tie a larger plan to workflow features that remove current blockers.

Confidence rule:

- High confidence when the client states repeated usage but defers annual until volume is clear.

Do-not-say:

- Do not push an annual plan as inherently better without volume math.

## Pattern: Executive Pressure-Test on Demo Access and Competition

Signals:

- An executive asks for hands-on trial, demo time, price clarity, and comparison with generic AI tools, manual workflows, or other vendors.
- The executive says the team needs to touch the tool before buying.

Likely meaning:

- The buyer is validating adoption risk, not just product features.

Recommended handling:

- Offer a scoped paid pilot or sandbox with success criteria.
- Prepare an alternative comparison around workflow, source discipline, structured output, and review process.
- Keep pricing and trial policy clean.

Confidence rule:

- High confidence when the executive controls the meeting flow and asks procurement or adoption questions.

Do-not-say:

- Do not avoid demo or trial policy.
- Do not claim generic superiority over other tools.

## Pattern: Strict Expert Team With Multiple Evaluators

Signals:

- Different participants test different areas: executive adoption, technical feasibility, legal risk, source validity, pricing, operations, and workflow fit.
- The team wants to validate on known projects or familiar cases where they already know the answer.

Likely meaning:

- A single polished pitch will not close the account. Each evaluator needs evidence for their domain.

Recommended handling:

- Build a role-based follow-up: executive summary, technical appendix, risk appendix, criteria intake, and pilot success metrics.
- Ask each evaluator what would make the output useful.

Confidence rule:

- High confidence when several participants ask domain-specific questions.

Do-not-say:

- Do not answer all stakeholders with one generic pitch.

## Pattern: Technical Evaluator Asking Feasibility or Compliance Depth Questions

Signals:

- Questions focus on constraints, approval paths, data sources, implementation blockers, cost triggers, risk flags, or what outcome is possible.
- The client says they do not need generic analysis and wants decision-grade constraints.

Likely meaning:

- The team must support feasibility direction and risk flags more than marketing language.

Recommended handling:

- Explain current constraints, possible paths, blockers, and confidence.
- Keep legal, regulatory, technical, or financial guarantees out of scope.
- Offer human-reviewed deeper analysis for high-risk items.

Confidence rule:

- High confidence when the client names specific constraints or review paths.

Do-not-say:

- Do not overpromise approval, legal, technical, or implementation outcomes.

## Pattern: Reverse Search or Pipeline Request as Custom Product Signal

Signals:

- The client asks for prospects, opportunities, matters, sites, accounts, or projects matching criteria rather than analysis of a known item.
- The client gives filters such as geography, industry, size, eligibility, risk profile, budget, or product fit.
- The client connects the output to outbound, acquisition, qualification, or pipeline generation.

Likely meaning:

- The opportunity may be larger than call analysis or single deliverables, but it is a custom data or workflow product with scope, compliance, and pricing risk.

Recommended handling:

- Treat as custom scope: criteria, geography, data sources, output fields, confidence, refresh cadence, and pricing.
- Separate known-case analysis from pipeline/list generation.

Confidence rule:

- High confidence when the client asks to identify eligible targets from criteria.

Do-not-say:

- Do not casually promise scraping, contact data, or outreach data without data-source and compliance review.

## Pattern: Quiet or Admin Participant Should Not Be Over-Profiled

Signals:

- A participant has logistical or minimal comments and no product, budget, legal, risk, or technical questions.
- Metadata identifies them as admin/support or non-decision.

Likely meaning:

- Include them in the attendee map but do not infer buying intent or blocker status.

Recommended handling:

- Mark insufficient evidence or logistical role.
- Focus sales analysis on decision maker and domain evaluators.

Confidence rule:

- High confidence when metadata and transcript both show no substantive decision role.

Do-not-say:

- Do not treat silence as skepticism without evidence.
