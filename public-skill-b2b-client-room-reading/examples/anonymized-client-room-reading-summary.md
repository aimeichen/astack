# Anonymized B2B Client Room Reading Demo

## 0. Privacy Note

This public demo is anonymized. Company names, people, locations, project names, and product-specific details have been replaced with generic labels.

Organizations are labeled as `Company 1 - contractor`, `Company 2 - contractor`, `Company 3 - prefab/manufacturing company`, and `Company 4 - developer`.

People are labeled as `Client A - Designer`, `Client B - Owner`, `Client C - Decision Maker`, and `Team Member 1 - PM` style references.

The goal is to demonstrate the room-reading method, not to reveal the original clients.

## 1. Executive Summary Across 4 Calls

| Call | Deal temperature | Main buying intent | Main blocker | Best next move |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Company 1 - contractor | Warm but early | Save research time and turn feasibility work into a client-facing recommendation | Source validity and editable deliverables | Send a revised deliverable, explain verification limits, and equip Client A to brief Client B |
| Company 2 - contractor | Warm, usage already proven | Use reports during lead qualification and sales preparation | Customer-facing pricing assumptions are not controllable enough | Provide editable output and support a custom pricing model before pushing annual conversion |
| Company 3 - prefab/manufacturing company | Exploratory partnership | Identify suitable opportunities and evaluate fit in bulk | Scope expands from analysis to data/list/referral workflow | Deliver a small sample list or fit report, then separately define monetization and compliance |
| Company 4 - developer | High-value but demanding | Validate technical feasibility, constraints, workflow fit, and team adoption | Needs proof, trial policy, pricing clarity, and comparison with generic AI tools | Run a scoped pilot on Project Site 1 and collect Client H's screening criteria |

Cross-call pattern:

- The strongest value proposition is not a polished report by itself. It is structured research that helps a team make a faster client or project decision.
- Conversion blockers sit in the last mile: editability, branding, pricing assumptions, section control, source confidence, and workflow integration.
- More expert clients do not accept broad AI claims. They ask about data sources, update logic, edge cases, assumptions, known-case validation, and comparison with their current workflow.
- The best sales motion is proof-driven: run one known case, compare against what the client already knows, then discuss a larger plan.

Main risks:

- Do not overpromise data freshness or approval outcomes.
- Do not casually accept broad custom scope.
- Do not let pricing sound improvised.
- Do not confuse the loudest speaker with the true decision maker.

## 2. Call 1 - Company 1 - Contractor

### Deal read

- Deal temperature: Warm but early.
- Client intent: Client A wants to reduce research time and create a clear client-facing recommendation.
- Main risk: A local source mismatch damaged confidence in the deliverable.
- Best next move: Send a revised deliverable and a concise owner-ready summary for Client B.
- Confidence: High.

### Client profile

#### Client A - Designer

- Role in call: Hands-on evaluator and internal champion.
- Communication style: detailed, candid, practical, and product-feedback oriented.
- Needs: fast research synthesis, editable deliverables, branded client-facing output, and source-confidence notes.
- Concerns: source freshness, timeline assumptions, cost assumptions, and whether the output can be shown to a client.
- What they respond to: precise acknowledgment, corrected output, editable files, and clear roadmap boundaries.
- What to avoid: broad claims that all data is always current.
- Confidence: High.

#### Client B - Owner

- Role in call: likely final economic decision maker, not directly present.
- Needs: ROI, repeat-use potential, and fit with company workflow.
- Handling: give Client A a short internal decision memo.
- Confidence: Medium.

## 8. Q&A Performance Patterns

Strong answers to repeat:

- Ask the client to explain the exact source or workflow issue.
- Offer revised output after a real mismatch.
- Provide editable deliverables when the client-facing format is a blocker.
- Explain assumptions instead of hiding them.
- Encourage comparison with alternatives without claiming generic superiority.
- Move complex buyers to a known-case pilot.

Weak answers to fix:

- Avoid saying data is always current.
- Avoid vague or shifting pricing.
- Avoid calling broad custom work easy.
- Avoid unlimited revision language.
- Avoid skipping a direct demo or trial-policy question.
- Avoid ambiguous visual indicators for pass/fail decisions.

## 12. Next-Call Prep Library

Price / budget:

- Prepare one clean pricing sheet with one-off, bundle, annual, and custom-scope options.

Timeline:

- State normal turnaround for standard scope and separate custom analysis.

Source validity:

- Use latest available sources, confidence flags, and human verification for high-impact assumptions.

Editable output:

- Explain current export options, fields that can be edited, and roadmap.

Custom assumptions:

- Separate fixed external costs, market assumptions, and client-specific assumptions.

Generic AI comparison:

- Compare structured workflow, source discipline, evidence, and review process.

Trial / demo:

- Offer a scoped pilot or sandbox with success criteria.

Custom screening:

- Ask for criteria, geography, source requirements, output fields, refresh cadence, and pricing expectations.

Do-not-say items:

- Do not guarantee approval or outcomes.
- Do not say sources are always current.
- Do not promise unlimited revisions.
- Do not say broad custom work is easy before scoping.
- Do not push a larger plan before usage volume is understood.
